The Berbera Axis: How Israel's Recognition of Somaliland Is Redrawing the Red Sea

From a thirty-five year diplomatic impasse to embassy openings in Jerusalem — mapping the realignment that has already fractured a Gulf coalition that stood for a decade.

On June 14, 2026, President Abdirahman Mohamed Abdullahi — known across Somaliland as Irro — walked into the President's Residence in Jerusalem and made history. It was the first state visit by any Somaliland head of state to any country, anywhere, since the territory declared independence from Somalia in 1991. President Isaac Herzog hosted him. A Knesset appearance and meetings with Israel's prime minister, foreign minister, and defence minister followed. By the end of the two-day trip, Somaliland had opened an embassy in Jerusalem, signed a Strategic Cooperation Declaration with Israel, received the Friends of Zion Award, and laid a wreath at the grave of Theodor Herzl — the founder of modern Zionism. And its president had announced, in an interview with Israeli media, that Somaliland is open to hosting an Israeli military base on its territory.

For a territory that has spent thirty-five years knocking on the world's door without a single country answering, this is vindication. For Somalia's federal government in Mogadishu, it is the gravest challenge to its sovereignty claim in a generation. And for the wider Horn of Africa and Red Sea security architecture, it is the visible tip of a realignment that has already, in six months, fractured a Gulf coalition that had stood for a decade.

This piece maps that realignment from every angle: Somaliland's gamble, Somalia's crisis, Ethiopia's opportunity, the UAE's quiet infrastructure campaign, Turkey's competing patronage, Saudi Arabia's surprising fault line, and the maritime security stakes that tie all of it back to the Bab el-Mandeb chokepoint this newsletter has been tracking since the Houthi naval ban this spring.

Section 01How we got here

The Israel-Somaliland track did not begin with the December 2025 recognition. Quiet discussions started roughly four years earlier, well before the Houthi crisis reshaped Red Sea calculus, and intensified after former president Muse Bihi Abdi visited Washington in March 2022. At that stage the ambition was modest — an exchange of trade offices or representative missions, the kind of unofficial presence the UAE, the United States, and Taiwan already maintained in Hargeisa.

Mossad spent years cultivating discreet, high-level relationships in Somaliland. Bashir Goth, Somaliland's representative to the United States, characterized this history as long-standing: "Israel was always on our side, on our radar." By April 2025, the pace of negotiations with Irro — Somaliland's newly elected president — was accelerating rapidly.

Everything changed on December 26, 2025. Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu and Foreign Minister Gideon Sa'ar signed a mutual declaration with Irro, making Israel the first UN member state ever to formally recognize Somaliland as an independent and sovereign nation. Netanyahu framed it explicitly as continuity with the Abraham Accords. Irro responded by announcing Somaliland would join the Abraham Accords framework itself, calling the moment a step toward regional and global peace.

The backlash was immediate and loud. Somalia's government called it a "deliberate attack" on its sovereignty. Thousands rallied in Mogadishu, waving Somali and Palestinian flags side by side. President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud flew to Turkey within days to coordinate a response with Erdogan, who called the recognition "illegitimate and unacceptable." Egypt, Turkey, the six-nation Gulf Cooperation Council, and the Saudi-based Organization of Islamic Cooperation all issued formal condemnations. The African Union joined Somalia in framing the move as a violation of international norms around territorial integrity.

Even Al-Shabaab found a propaganda opening. The group — which has essentially no operational presence in Somaliland — vowed to "fight" any Israeli attempt to "claim or use parts of Somaliland," framing the recognition as evidence of Western and Israeli encroachment. The group doesn't need a foothold in Hargeisa to use this narrative; it needs a storyline, and Israel just handed it one.

Section 02What Israel actually wants

Strip away the symbolism and Israel's interest in Somaliland is straightforwardly geographic. Somaliland sits roughly 30 kilometers south of the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — the chokepoint through which an enormous share of traffic between the Red Sea and the Indian Ocean must pass, and the same chokepoint the Houthis have spent two years demonstrating they can threaten.

Recognition gives Israel its first diplomatic foothold in the Red Sea Basin since relations with Eritrea collapsed in 2020, and since Sudan's normalization track froze when civil war broke out there in 2023. Israeli Defence Minister Israel Katz confirmed during Irro's June visit that Israel had been cooperating with Somaliland "under the radar in a series of operations" for years, and expected this to reach "new heights." The Telegraph reported in May 2026 that 50 Somaliland special forces had already completed military training in Tel Aviv.

Military base: the gap between denial and reality. The official Somaliland position is that there is no Israeli military base and no negotiations underway — Somaliland's Defence Minister Mohamed Yusuf Ali told Reuters this while attending a business conference in Tel Aviv. But the picture emerging from multiple independent sources is more complicated. Drop Site News, citing regional officials including a Somaliland official, reported Israel has established an intelligence presence at Berbera Airport. Commercial satellite imagery shows significant expansion of military infrastructure at and around Berbera air base over the past three months. CNN reported Somaliland provided covert basing access to Israeli aircraft during the 2026 Iran conflict for long-range missions. A retired Israeli general stated publicly that "the Israeli navy has been operating in the area." When Somaliland's president was asked directly about a military base, he said: "I cannot rule out." Somaliland's foreign policy adviser told Channel 14 that security cooperation had begun. Katz confirmed cooperation "under the radar in a series of activities that will remain secret." What is being officially denied and what is operationally underway appear to be two different things.

The payoff, if a full base proceeds, is positional: a forward location on a Soviet-built 2.6-mile runway capable of receiving heavy transport aircraft, fighter jets, and drone platforms for round-the-clock surveillance of the Bab el-Mandeb — 260 kilometers from Yemen's coast. Israel reportedly used covert Somaliland facilities during the 2026 Iran conflict as a stop for long-range flights. The Berbera airport sits adjacent to a DP World deepwater commercial port and a separate UAE naval base established in 2017. The infrastructure is already there. The question is whether the political framework around it will ever be publicly acknowledged.

Section 03Somaliland's bet: all in, no hedge

For Hargeisa, this is not a hedge — it's a wager that the international system's resistance to recognizing breakaway states can be broken by a sufficiently prestigious first mover, and that everything else follows from there. The embassy-in-Jerusalem choice reinforces this. The seven other countries with embassies in Jerusalem rather than Tel Aviv are, almost without exception, states whose foreign policy already runs through Washington and through the most pro-Israel diplomatic bloc available. By joining that group, Somaliland is signaling that its bet isn't just on Israel — it's on the broader constellation of relationships that recognition might eventually unlock.

The Strategic Cooperation Declaration signed during the June visit formalizes this across economy, security, technology, investment, and community development. Direct flights between Israel and Somaliland — announced as coming "very soon" — will make the relationship physically visible in a way that no diplomatic document can. There is no attempt here to keep Mogadishu, Ankara, or Cairo on side. The strategy is to make the relationship with Israel and its allies valuable enough, fast enough, that the cost of opposition becomes irrelevant.

Section 04Somalia: an existential framing, and a real one

It would be easy to read Mogadishu's reaction as reflexive. But the Somali government's framing of this as an existential threat isn't hyperbole from its perspective. Somalia's Ali Omar, State Minister for Foreign Affairs, told Al Jazeera that his government's "gravest fear" was that Somaliland would be drawn into Israel's regional conflicts: "This interference imports conflict into a region that has already suffered enough." President Mohamud described the recognition as "not a recognition but a trap" intended to draw Somalia into a conflict that was neither its own nor in its national interest.

The structural risk is that this issue now sits permanently on the agenda of Somalia's most important external relationships. Every interaction with Turkey, Egypt, the GCC, or the OIC will have a "what are you doing about Somaliland" component. Every Israeli or UAE step forward in Hargeisa becomes a fresh occasion for protest — channeling political energy and capital toward a fight Mogadishu cannot easily win through diplomacy alone.

Data context from our dataset: Somaliland recorded 283 security incidents and 152 fatalities in the Somali OSINT Brief master dataset (1997-present). Of 106 incidents in 2024 — the highest single-year count — 85 resulted in fatalities, a significant jump from prior years. Political violence (111 incidents total) and strategic developments (90) dominate the type breakdown. The SSC-Khaatumo region — disputed between Somaliland and Puntland — recorded 85 incidents in 2023 alone (890 fatalities), its most violent year on record.

Section 05The piece nobody's watching closely enough: Puntland

Puntland wasn't part of the June headlines, but its position is structurally important. Puntland has spent decades resisting both Somaliland's independence claims — particularly over the disputed Sool, Sanaag, and Cayn regions — and Mogadishu's efforts to centralize federal authority. An internationally legitimized, increasingly well-resourced Somaliland strengthens Hargeisa's hand in exactly the border disputes Puntland cares most about.

Puntland also has its own separate security relationships — counter-piracy and counter-ISIS cooperation with the US and UAE — that don't run through Mogadishu. The Washington Institute noted that the UAE, following Somalia's retaliatory cancellation of port security deals, retained its relationship with Bosaso port (under Puntland control) regardless of Mogadishu's announcement — since Mogadishu doesn't actually control Bosaso. This is the kind of structural reality that makes Mogadishu's sovereign claims more complicated than they look from the outside.

Section 06Ethiopia: the quiet structural winner

If there's one actor that wins regardless of how the Israel-Somalia dimension of this story resolves, it's Ethiopia. Under its memorandum of understanding with Somaliland, Addis Ababa secured a 50-year lease on 20 kilometers of Somaliland's coastline — giving landlocked Ethiopia its first Red Sea access since Eritrea's independence. Somalia's response was sharp: recalled its ambassador from Addis Ababa and passed a law nullifying the deal. None of that has stopped construction.

Israel's interest in Berbera dovetails directly with Ethiopia's. An efficient Berbera corridor reduces Ethiopia's dependence on Djibouti, where Chinese influence over port infrastructure has long worried Addis Ababa's Western-aligned partners. The broader picture analysts describe is of Ethiopia embedded in a UAE-Israel axis: UAE capital and infrastructure investment, Israeli cooperation on technology, water management, cyber security, and intelligence — and all of it indirectly giving Addis Ababa more leverage in its long-running dispute with Egypt over the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam.

Section 07The UAE: the indispensable builder

If Israel provides the diplomatic breakthrough and Ethiopia provides the landlocked demand, the UAE provides the physical infrastructure that makes any of this real. DP World's investment in Berbera — roughly $442 million — has expanded the port's capacity from 150,000 to 500,000 TEUs, transforming a modest regional port into a serious logistics hub. Beyond the port itself, the UAE has layered in a "Green Berbera Vision" solar initiative and funded a $7.5 million social housing project.

Notably, the UAE itself has not formally recognized Somaliland — but its acceptance of Somaliland passports as of early 2026 amounts to practical recognition without the diplomatic cost of a formal declaration. And while the GCC issued a collective statement condemning Israel's recognition, the UAE has not issued a standalone national condemnation — a distinction that matters. The UAE was not, in the language of the Atlantic Council analysis, "one of the few Arab League members that did not join the chorus denouncing Israel's recognition." Abu Dhabi's posture signals careful balancing of Somaliland engagement against broader Gulf dynamics.

Section 08Turkey: the rival patron, doubling down

While Israel and the UAE build out Somaliland, Turkey has spent the same period deepening what is already one of the most extensive foreign footprints in any African capital. Turkey's presence in Mogadishu includes an embassy on the scale of its Afghan compound, a military training base, hospitals, infrastructure projects, and the Turkish-operated Mogadishu airport, built at roughly $300 million.

In the same window as the Israel-Somaliland recognition, Erdogan and Mohamud announced a bilateral accord for Turkey's first spaceport — a roughly 900-square-kilometer site on Somalia's Indian Ocean coast. Turkey also confirmed plans to begin offshore energy drilling off Somalia's coast in 2026. The result is a patron-versus-patron geography: Turkey embedded in federal Somalia's military, economic, and space infrastructure in the south, while Israel, the UAE, and Ethiopia build out security and logistics infrastructure in Somaliland in the north.

Section 09Saudi Arabia: the variable nobody priced in

Here is the development that should reshape how this story gets told. On December 30, 2025 — four days after the Israel-Somaliland recognition — a Saudi-led coalition airstrike hit the southern Yemeni port of Mukalla, targeting a UAE-backed weapons shipment. The timing was extraordinary: the UAE-backed Southern Transitional Council had just declared a breakaway "South Arabia," meaning the map briefly contained both a newly recognized Somaliland and a newly declared South Arabia — two pro-UAE, potentially pro-Israeli entities on opposite sides of the Bab el-Mandeb.

Saudi Arabia treated this as a "red line" and, in an unusually direct move in Gulf diplomacy, accused the UAE by name of actions risking destabilization of the anti-Houthi coalition. Riyadh gave Abu Dhabi a 24-hour ultimatum. Within 48 hours, Emirati troops had evacuated remaining positions along Yemen's southern coast. A military and political project the UAE had built over a decade ended in two days.

The open question: Does Saudi Arabia view the Berbera Axis — Israeli basing access, UAE port investment, Somaliland recognition — as part of the same pattern of overreach it just crushed in Yemen? If Riyadh draws that line, the entire calculus around Somaliland's external backing changes. If it treats them as unrelated, the Berbera Axis proceeds largely unimpeded by Gulf rivalries. This is the single biggest swing factor in this entire analysis.

Section 10Egypt, the Arab League, and the African Union

Egypt coordinated the joint condemnation alongside Somalia, Turkey, and Djibouti. Egypt's stake here isn't abstract: Cairo is acutely sensitive to precedents around territorial fragmentation given its own anxieties about the GERD dispute and Ethiopia's broader regional ascent — an ascent the Berbera corridor materially assists. Egypt is opposed to the Israel-Somaliland recognition on Somalia's behalf, while also independently wary of the Ethiopian gains that same recognition enables.

The African Union's response follows naturally from its founding commitment to colonial-era borders — a principle that, if it erodes for Somalia, erodes for the dozens of AU member states with their own internal separatist movements. The AU "strongly condemned and rejected" Israel's recognition and urged immediate revocation. Expect continued rhetorical escalation, but limited concrete action.

Section 11The United States: backing from a comfortable distance

Washington's posture has been to let Israel run point while keeping its own options open. The US representative at the UN Security Council defended Israel's recognition, saying it "has the same right to conduct diplomatic relations as any other sovereign State" — while adding there had been "no change in American policy" toward Somaliland. Trump, when pressed, said he wasn't ready to follow. That gap is informative: Washington is comfortable with Israel testing the waters without committing the weight of US recognition, and without forcing an immediate reckoning with Saudi Arabia.

Meanwhile, the US has continued its own, separate counterterrorism track in Somalia, including airstrikes against ISIS positions in the northeast in early 2026 — a reminder that Washington's security relationship with federal Somalia operates on its own track, largely insulated from the Somaliland question for now.

Section 12Iran, the Houthis, and the maritime stakes

This is where the story connects back to the Bab al-Mandeb analysis this newsletter ran earlier this year. As of early 2026, Red Sea shipping had cautiously begun returning to Suez routes. But the prevailing assessment is that this represents a structured pause under tension rather than genuine de-escalation. The risk for Somalia is layered: if Israel's deepening presence in Berbera becomes a visible forward platform for anti-Houthi operations, it raises the attractiveness of Somaliland's coastline as a target for Houthi or Iranian retaliation.

Houthi leader Abdel-Malek al-Houthi warned directly that any Israeli presence in Somaliland would be considered "a military target." If this calculus extends to broader Gulf of Aden environments, Somalia's own ports and coastal infrastructure — which Somalia has no ability to defend from this kind of threat — sit in the zone of indirect consequence.

In other words: Somalia doesn't need to be a party to the Israel-Somaliland relationship to feel its consequences. The maritime security environment that determines how much it costs to get food, fuel, and goods into Mogadishu and Kismayo is increasingly shaped by a set of relationships Somalia has no say in and no ability to de-escalate.

Section 13What to watch next

Watch 01 — Mogadishu's response to the embassy opening
Given the pattern established after recognition and after the foreign minister's visit, expect renewed protests, a fresh round of coordinated condemnation with Turkey and Egypt, and likely an appeal to the African Union or UN bodies framing the embassy as a further escalation. Somalia's Ali Omar has already described the June visit as evidence that his "gravest fear" is being realized.
Watch 02 — Military base: from denial to operational reality
The most important thread to track. Commercial satellite imagery already shows significant expansion of military infrastructure at Berbera air base over the past three months. Drop Site News reported an Israeli intelligence presence confirmed by multiple officials. CNN confirmed Somaliland provided covert basing to Israeli aircraft during the 2026 Iran conflict. Somaliland's defence minister called it "rumours" — from a business conference in Tel Aviv. The president said "I cannot rule out." Watch for: formal base announcement, flight path data, and whether the Houthis move Berbera into their explicit targeting calculus.
Watch 03 — Whether Saudi Arabia extends its Yemen red line to Berbera
The single biggest swing factor in this analysis. If Riyadh treats the Berbera Axis as continuous with the "South Arabia" overreach it crushed in 48 hours, the entire calculus around Somaliland's external backing changes. If it treats them as unrelated, the Berbera Axis proceeds largely unimpeded.
Watch 04 — Houthi posture toward Berbera specifically
So far, Houthi targeting has focused on shipping and direct Israeli/Saudi/UAE assets. Whether Somaliland's coastline becomes an explicit part of that targeting calculus would mark a significant escalation with direct consequences for Somalia's coastal security, independent of Mogadishu's political position on any of this.
Watch 05 — Puntland's alignment
Less prominent in the headlines, but structurally important: how Puntland positions itself relative to the Sool/Sanaag/Cayn disputes as Somaliland gains international legitimacy and resources will shape Somalia's internal federal balance regardless of how the Israel question resolves.
Watch 06 — Direct flights and commercial normalization
Irro announced direct flights between Israel and Somaliland coming "very soon." When those launch, they make the relationship physically visible in a way no diplomatic document can — and will draw another round of condemnation from Somalia and the Arab League. The launch date and the carriers involved will signal how fast both sides want to move.
